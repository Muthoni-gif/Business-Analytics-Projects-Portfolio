Analysis 01

Where is Attrition Happening?

Breaking down attrition across departments and job roles reveals that the problem is not evenly distributed — it's concentrated in specific pockets of the organisation.

Attrition Rate by Department

Sales has nearly double the attrition rate of R&D

Attrition by Job Role

Sales Representatives are the highest-risk role at 39.8%
⚠ Risk Alert

Sales Representatives have a 39.8% attrition rate — nearly 1 in 2 leave. This is the single highest-risk role in the dataset.

📊 Context

R&D Department has the lowest rate at 13.8% despite having the largest headcount — suggesting structure and growth paths matter.

✓ Opportunity

If Sales attrition dropped to match R&D levels, the company could retain approximately 30+ additional employees per year.

Analysis 02

When Do Employees Leave?

Timing matters as much as who leaves. Understanding attrition by age and tenure reveals the critical window where intervention has the highest impact.

Attrition Count by Age Group

Employees aged 26–35 account for the largest share of leavers

Attrition by Years at Company

The first 2 years are the most critical retention window
⚠ Critical Window

Over 40% of all attrition happens within the first 2 years. Poor onboarding and unmet expectations are the likely drivers.

📊 Age Factor

The 26–35 age group — typically early-career professionals — show the highest absolute attrition, suggesting career growth opportunities need improvement.

✓ Good News

Employees who stay beyond 5 years show dramatically lower attrition, indicating strong long-term loyalty once the early hurdle is cleared.

Analysis 03

Why Are They Leaving?

Cross-referencing attrition with satisfaction scores, work-life balance ratings, and income levels surfaces the underlying drivers behind the numbers.

Job Satisfaction vs Attrition

Low satisfaction = higher exit rate

Work-Life Balance Rating

Leavers vs Stayers comparison

Monthly Income Distribution

Leavers consistently earn less
⚠ Satisfaction Signal

Employees with satisfaction score 1 (Lowest) are 2.6× more likely to leave than those rating 4 (Highest).

⚠ Compensation Gap

Employees who left had an average monthly income of $4,787 vs $6,833 for those who stayed — a 30% gap.

📊 Multi-Factor

No single factor explains all attrition. The combination of low pay + low satisfaction + poor work-life balance creates the highest exit risk.

Analysis 04

The Overtime Effect

One of the strongest predictors of attrition in this dataset is overtime. The correlation is stark and actionable.

Attrition Rate: Overtime vs No Overtime

Employees working overtime are 3× more likely to leave

Overtime by Department

Sales & HR have the highest overtime exposure
⚠ Strongest Predictor

Overtime is the single strongest predictor of attrition in this dataset. 30.5% of overtime workers leave vs only 10.4% of non-overtime workers.

📊 Business Cost

With average replacement cost estimated at 50–200% of annual salary, reducing overtime-driven attrition could save the company hundreds of thousands per year.

✓ Clear Action

This is the most actionable finding in the analysis — HR can immediately audit departments with high overtime and implement workload redistribution policies.

💬 How to Present This in an Interview

The Story Behind the Numbers

Here's how I'd walk an interviewer through this project in 90 seconds:

1

The business question: "I wanted to understand why 16% of this company's workforce was leaving and what HR could do about it — not just describe the problem, but find an actionable lever."

2

The key finding: "Overtime was the single biggest predictor of attrition — overtime workers leave at 3× the rate of others, and it's concentrated in Sales and HR."

3

The business implication: "If the company reduced overtime-driven exits by just 30%, and using a conservative replacement cost of 50% of salary, that's an estimated $500K+ in savings annually."

4

What I'd do next: "I'd layer in exit interview data to validate these findings, and build a predictive model in Python to flag at-risk employees before they decide to leave."
