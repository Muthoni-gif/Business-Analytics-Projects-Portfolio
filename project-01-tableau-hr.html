Key Findings

What the Data Reveals

Four critical insights emerged — each pointing to a specific, actionable lever the business can pull to reduce attrition.

⚠ Strongest Predictor
3×

Employees working overtime leave at 30.5% vs only 10.4% for non-overtime workers — the single biggest attrition driver in the dataset.

📊 Highest Risk Role
39.8%

Sales Representatives have the highest attrition rate — nearly 1 in 2 leave. This is more than double the company average of 16.1%.

💰 Compensation Gap
$2,046

Employees who left earned an average of $4,787/month vs $6,833 for those who stayed — a 30% pay gap strongly linked to exits.

🎯 Highest Risk Age
18–25

The youngest group has the highest attrition rate at 35.8% — early-career employees need stronger onboarding, mentorship and growth paths.

💬 How to Present This in an Interview

The Story Behind the Numbers

1

The business question: "I wanted to understand why 16% of this company's workforce was leaving and find an actionable lever — not just describe the problem."

2

The key finding: "Overtime was the single biggest predictor — overtime workers leave at 3× the rate, concentrated in Sales and HR."

3

The business implication: "Reducing overtime-driven exits by 30%, at a conservative 50% replacement cost, represents $500K+ in annual savings."

4

What I'd do next: "Layer in exit interview data, then build a Python model to flag at-risk employees before they decide to leave."
